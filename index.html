A Proposed New High School and the Geography of Enrollment

by Rob Reid
30 October 2022

In late September, several local news outlets reported on a Chicago Public Schools (CPS) board meeting vote narrowly supporting acquisition of land intended for a proposed new Near South Side high school costing $120 million. The proposed school has community champions. But WBEZ cited a confidential memo by top CPS officials concerned that competition from a new school would accelerate the decline of nearby schools, while referencing “deep concerns” expressed by the school district data manager in an internal memo from the previous year.

Earlier in the month, CPS released annual enrollment data underscoring concerns about systemwide enrollment decline, while the state representative who secured $50 million from the state in support of the school expressed concerns about lack of community input in the planning process.

With key votes in support of CPS capital funding still ahead, it's critical to take a closer look at the presumably concerning data. Many factors should be considered, including an analysis of budget, teacher availability, and access to school programming such as population-specific language offerings.
The following analysis focuses more narrowly on enrollment in an attempt to illuminate the geographical component of the story: where would students for a proposed new school come from? This analysis, in conjunction with more targeted conversations with local stakeholders, could advance an investigation of the proposed new school's potential impact on existing schools.

A Question of Geography

  1. Looking at year over year change in CPS enrollment by community area shows declining enrollment in most areas of the city.

The five-year enrollment trends for the three open enrollment schools with boundaries overlapping the new school's proposed boundaries- Tilden, Phillips, and Wells- shows that all three areas are declining in enrollment.

Complicating the picture a bit, Tilden has shown a rebound in enrollment in the past year despite the overall downward trend over five years.

3) Visualization of enrollment at these schools as a percent of ideal capacity, as measured by CPS’s 2021-22 Space Utilization report. Note that CPS defines “underutilized” as under 70% of ideal capacity. Tilden, Phillips, and Wells range between 11% to 36% utilization.

This could help illuminate whether trends in the local CPS elementary school-aged population point to a need for future capacity at local high schools.

Questions Remain

These analyses raise some interesting questions. Related to graphic #2 above:

  • Tilden's enrollment has rebounded 20% (adding 36 students) in the past year, bucking a trend of enrollment decline amongst small schools. What factors are causing this, and could it provide lessons for enrollment recovery?
  • What would make these existing high schools more appealing?

Related to graphic #4 above:

  • Is elementary school enrollment truly reflective of high school enrollment potential, or could the new school potentially attract high school aged students who had never been in CPS? To get a sense of broader demographics outside of CPS I built a block assignment file for the school boundaries as a first step in anchoring the demographic analysis to 2020 census data.
  • Given the slightly increasing enrollment at National Teachers Academy elementary school, did 2018 discussions about converting this existing school to a high school have merit compared to the current proposal for a new building?

Seeking Answers and Context

Schools chief Pedro Martinez is on the record as promising both an investment in the existing schools and a new school:

“People have talked about this as an 'or,'' so either you build a new school or invest in old schools,” Martinez said. “I’ve never seen this as an ‘or.’ For me, it’s always been an ‘and.’

What, concretely, will CPS offer to Tilden, Dunbar, and Wells? What do these schools need to succeed?

I've assembled a list of Local School Council members at the three schools and believe that interviews with community members, parents, staff, and students in these groups would bring more context to the data and elevate past and current advocacy efforts made on behalf of the declining schools in the Near South Side area.
